02 / six case studies
FRAME / 01 — UI & LAYOUT

Syntax Real Estate

UI DesignLayoutWeb

Real estate sites usually drown buyers in information before they've even seen a house. I designed a landing page that leads with the emotional hook — "find your perfect home" — and only then earns its way into listings, pricing, and trust.

What I focused on

  • A simple hero: bold headline, one supporting line, one action. No competing for attention.
  • A scrollable "Popular Properties" row so price and type are visible at a glance.
  • A trust-building CTA block placed after the listings, not before — once someone's browsed, that's the moment to pitch credibility.
  • A quieter blog section at the bottom that supports SEO without competing with the main goal: get people searching.

Where I'd take it further

Testing property card density at 3 vs. 4 visible, and a sticky search bar so the primary action stays in reach while scrolling.

FRAME / 02 — UI & MOBILE

Daily UI — Sign In / Sign Up

UI DesignMobileAuth Flow

The Daily UI brief was just "design this screen." I treated it as a chance to design a complete pair, because in real products, sign-in and sign-up never exist alone.

What I focused on

  • Identical structure across both screens — same spacing, button style, login order — so the pair reads as one continuous flow.
  • A single accent colour used only on the primary action, so there's never a question of what to tap.
  • Social logins placed below the form, since most returning users sign in with email first.

Where I'd take it further

Inline validation before submit, and reconsidering "Forgot password" placement — it's easy to miss next to the checkbox right now.

FRAME / 03 — E-COMMERCE

Checkout Flow

UI DesignE-commerceMobile

Checkout is one of the highest-stakes screens in any product — it's where carts get abandoned. The job here was to remove every bit of friction between "almost done" and "done."

What I focused on

  • Order summary stays visible above the form so the total is never a mystery.
  • Tabs across the top (Order details / Delivery / Payment) signal this is step 3 of a flow, not a one-off form.
  • A moody product photo keeps the brand premium without hurting form legibility, thanks to a dark overlay and high-contrast fields.

Where I'd take it further

A visible security cue near the card fields — a lock icon or "secure checkout" line. Trust signals matter more here than almost anywhere else in a flow.

Sunshine — Volunteer Sign-Up Flow

WireframingUX FlowReal Client Problem

The problem: Sunshine had a volunteer interest form buried three taps deep on their Instagram bio link, with no way to tell people what they'd actually be signing up for. Interest was there — people commented on posts wanting to help — but it wasn't converting into sign-ups.

My approach: Before touching any screen, I mapped the flow a volunteer would need: see what we do → understand the roles → give minimal info → get a clear next step. Wireframing first let me test that structure without getting distracted by colour or imagery.

Decisions in the wireframe

  • Landing screen leads with what volunteering involves, not a form — people commit faster once they know what they're signing up for.
  • Sign-up screen asks for three fields only, plus one choice: which area they want to help with. Fewer fields, faster completion.
  • Confirmation screen gives an immediate next step ("Add to Calendar") instead of just a thank-you message, so interest doesn't go cold before the next event.

Outcome & next step

This wireframe is currently being built out into final UI and handed to the Sunshine team to replace the bio-link form. Next: usability-test the role-selection step with 3–4 real volunteers before finalising visuals.

Ecodiversified — Event & Donation Flow

WireframingUX FlowReal Client Problem

The problem: Ecodiversified runs clean-up events and relies on small donations to fund them, but the two asks were tangled together — people interested in attending an event had no clean way to just give money instead, and vice versa.

My approach: I split the two intents into a single flow with a fork, instead of two separate competing CTAs. Browsing events is the entry point either way; donating becomes one tap from any event, not a separate hunt.

Decisions in the wireframe

  • Events screen uses a simple grid so someone can scan dates and locations in seconds, with one clear way to see more.
  • Event detail puts location and date as scannable tags up top, and offers RSVP as the primary action — donation lives one tap away, not competing for the same button.
  • Support screen uses preset amounts (with one highlighted as a suggested default) plus a custom field, so giving takes one tap instead of typing for most people.

Outcome & next step

This wireframe is going to the Ecodiversified team for feedback before I move into visual design. Next: confirm which donation amount should be the suggested default based on past giving data.
